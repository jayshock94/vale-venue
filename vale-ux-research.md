## THE MARKET

Utah County event venues are dominated by LDS wedding receptions.
In this market the ceremony happens at the temple and the reception is the main gathering event.
The venue carries the full emotional weight of the day.
This is not a ceremony venue — it is a reception venue, and that shapes everything.

Most searches start on mobile via Pinterest and Instagram.
Google search narrows the list.
The actual inquiry usually happens on desktop.
The decision to reach out happens on mobile, often within seconds of landing.

---

## THREE USER TYPES

### User 1 — Brides and Couples

Primary question: "Does this match the vision in my head, and can we actually afford it?"

Device: Mobile for discovery (Pinterest, Instagram). Desktop for inquiry.

Booking timeline: 9 to 18 months out for Saturday dates.
LDS couples move faster — temple sealing times drive the date.
They need a reception venue locked as soon as the temple date is confirmed.
Weekend Saturdays book 2 to 4 months out at most local venues.

What they need to see before they will reach out:
- Real photos from actual weddings at that venue. Not styled shoots. Not stock.
  They specifically check Instagram to see what real events looked like.
- Transparent pricing or at least a starting rate.
- Capacity that fits their guest list.
- A bridal suite and groom suite clearly stated.
- Catering policy stated explicitly. They assume they cannot bring their own caterer unless told otherwise.
- How far the venue is from the Provo City Center Temple.

Friction points that cause them to leave without contacting:
- No pricing visible. Forces an inquiry just to find out if it is in budget.
- Stock photos or overly perfect images.
- No availability indicator. They have a date in mind and cannot check without calling.
- Contact forms with no response time stated. They fill out 5 of these and go with whoever replies first.
- A site that feels built for one kind of couple and they cannot see themselves in it.

Search phrases used:
"wedding reception venue Provo Utah"
"modern wedding venue Utah County"
"reception venue near Provo City Center Temple"
"dry hire wedding venue Provo"
"event space wedding Wasatch Front"

LDS market context:
The ceremony and reception are almost always separate events.
The sealing happens at the temple. Guests who are not members wait outside.
The reception is where everyone gathers. It carries the full emotional weight of the day.
Couples are searching for a beautiful reception space, not a ceremony venue.

---

### User 2 — Corporate Event Planners

Primary question: "Can I show this to my boss and have it approved without explaining anything?"

Device: Desktop. Often comparing 3 to 5 venues in tabs simultaneously.
They are reading carefully, not scrolling and dreaming.

Booking timeline: 4 to 8 weeks for smaller meetings.
2 to 6 months for retreats, training days, or company events with travel.
Mon to Thu is when corporate clients want dates — lowest pricing, best availability.

What they need to see before they will reach out:
- AV setup and tech specs: projector, screen, microphone, HDMI.
- Capacity for different layouts: theater, classroom, reception.
- Parking — specifically whether there is enough for a team of 20 to 50.
- Fast and professional email response. They are booking 3+ venues simultaneously and drop the slow ones.
- Day-of contact person named and accessible.
- Published pricing. Corporate events often require budget sign-off before a tour, so hidden pricing is a hard stop.

Friction points:
- Site looks like a wedding venue only. Corporate planners self-select out when every photo is floral arches.
- No AV information visible anywhere.
- Vague or undisclosed pricing.
- Complicated inquiry forms. They need a quick yes or no on date and capacity before anything else.

Search phrases used:
"corporate event venue Provo Utah"
"meeting space Utah County"
"event space for company retreat Wasatch Front"
"training room rental Provo"
"business event venue near BYU"

### User 3 — Birthday and Celebration Hosts

Primary question: "Is this place going to impress my guests without destroying my budget?"

Device: Mobile. Often discovers via Instagram or TikTok.
Sees a venue tagged in someone's birthday video, taps through, books or bounces within minutes.

Booking timeline: 2 to 6 weeks.
Most impulsive buyer of the three types.
They find a space, check the date is open, and book quickly or move on.
Shorter path from landing to booking = higher conversion.

What they need to see before they will reach out:
- Price. This user will not inquire without at least a starting number.
- Photos of celebrations that look like theirs. Not weddings. Not corporate.
- Space for a dance floor.
- Kitchen access for their caterer.
- Easy inquiry process. If the form has more than 5 fields they leave.
- Capacity listed.

Friction points:
- Feeling like a second-class booking. If the entire site is built for weddings and they are planning a birthday, they feel like a nuisance.
- Minimums or deposit requirements not stated upfront.
- The venue feeling too formal for a party.
- No clear hourly or short-block option. They often do not need a full day and resent paying for one.
- Site not mobile-friendly.

Search phrases used:
"party venue Provo Utah"
"birthday party venue Utah County"
"event space rental Provo"
"quinceañera venue Utah County"
"rent a space for party Provo"

Note on hourly pricing:
The hourly option ($300 for first 3 hours + $200 each additional) is directly positioned for this user
but it is buried. The site leads with full-day packages. For this audience the short-block option
should be visible and clearly framed. A birthday for 60 people at $500 for 4 hours is an easy yes.

## WHAT ALL THREE USER TYPES NEED — SHARED

Regardless of event type, every venue seeker needs:

1. Real photos of actual events at that specific space.
   Not styled shoots, not stock.
   They use Instagram to verify what events really looked like.

2. Transparent pricing or at minimum a starting rate.
   Every user type named hidden pricing as the top friction point.
   Found repeatedly in reviews on The Knot, WeddingWire, and Reddit.

3. Capacity stated clearly.
   Ideally in multiple formats: seated dinner vs flowing reception vs standing.
   The same space means different things to different events.

4. Catering policy stated explicitly.
   BYO, preferred vendor list, or restrictions.
   Every client asks this before inquiring. Ambiguity kills conversions.

5. A clear, fast path to check availability or contact.
   Long forms, slow responses, or no response timeline cause drop-off
   at exactly the highest-intent moment.

6. Social proof — even one or two named testimonials.
   Reviews are how all three types move a venue from "interesting" to "worth contacting."

## MOBILE-FIRST REQUIREMENT

Utah County venue searches happen predominantly on mobile.
LDS couples are often on their phones the moment they leave the temple,
showing family venue options on a walk-through.
Birthday and celebration hosts discover venues on Instagram and TikTok and tap through immediately.

Any pricing, CTA, or capacity information that requires scrolling
past the first screen on mobile is likely being missed.
The entire site must be evaluated phone-first.

## MARKET PATTERNS — WHAT THE RESEARCH SHOWS

### Baseline expectations (what every competitor does — table stakes)
- Real interior event photos at or near the hero
- "Schedule a Tour" is the primary CTA across all 8 venues. Users expect it above the fold.
- Pricing transparent or partially visible. 4 of 5 local competitors show rates.
- BYO catering is the assumed model. No local competitor offers in-house catering.
- Mobile-responsive site. This is the floor, not a differentiator.
- A vendor or preferred supplier list. Clients expect photographer, caterer, and decorator recommendations.

### Common gaps (what most venues are missing)
- No local competitor has an availability calendar.
  Every one forces the user to reach out just to check if a date is open.
  This is friction at exactly the highest-intent moment.
- Catering policy is unclear or buried on most local sites,
  despite being one of the first questions every client asks.
- Identical language across all local venues.
  Every site says "unforgettable," "perfect backdrop," "seamless experience."
  None of them sound like a distinct person or place.
- Owner presence is weak across the market.
  Only La Fete (Wade) makes the owner a visible feature.
  Clients book people, not rooms.
- No local site has a thorough FAQ page.
  Users generally have the same questions. An FAQ reduces pre-inquiry friction significantly.
- The celebration market has no dedicated path on any local site.
  Birthday and anniversary hosts feel like second-class bookings everywhere.

## UX PRINCIPLES THAT APPLY TO THIS SITE

### Selective Attention
Users filter out everything that is not relevant to what they came to do.
They are not reading the page — they are scanning for the specific thing they need.
Each user type has a different thing they are scanning for:
- Brides: photos, price, bridal suite
- Corporate: AV, capacity, price
- Celebration hosts: price, photos of parties, short-block option

Implications for design:
- The thing each user type needs most must be findable without reading every line.
- Competing elements at the same visual weight force the user to do work they will not do.
- Elements styled like ads or banners get ignored. Trust signals need to look like content.
- Change blindness: if something important changes or appears, it needs a strong visual cue.

### Goal Gradient Effect
Users work harder and faster the closer they feel to completing something.
The site must create a sense of progress toward booking.

Implications for design:
- The inquiry form should feel like the natural end of a journey, not a cold start.
- Users who have scrolled through pricing, seen amenities, and read testimonials
  are closer to their goal and more likely to convert. The CTA at the end of that journey
  should reflect that — it is not the same as the CTA at the top of the page.
- Providing even artificial progress signals increases completion rate.
  A form that shows "3 quick questions" feels more completable than a blank form.

### Progressive Disclosure
Show the right amount of information at the right moment.
Not everything at once. Not too little too soon.

Implications for design:
- The home page is the surface layer. Each inner page goes deeper on one topic.
- A user who navigates to the pricing page already knows what The Vale is.
  They do not need to be re-introduced to it. Start from where the home page left off.
- The gallery filter tabs are a good example of progressive disclosure done right —
  they let users self-select the event type they care about before showing everything.

### Von Restorff Effect (Isolation Effect)
When multiple similar objects are present, the one that differs is remembered.
One thing can be made to stand out by making everything else recede.

Implications for design:
- Gold is used sparingly for exactly this reason. It should appear on the thing that matters most.
- The peak pricing row standing out in gold works because the rest of the table does not.
- If too many things compete for attention, nothing stands out.

### Fitts's Law
The time to reach a target is a function of the distance and size of the target.
Smaller targets and targets far from the user's attention require more effort.

Implications for design:
- Primary CTAs should be large enough and close enough to where the user's eye already is.
- On mobile, touch targets must be at minimum 48px (Material 3 standard).
- The most important action on any screen should not require the user to hunt for it.

## CONTENT THE SITE NEEDS TO FUNCTION

### Priority (highest friction when missing)
- Real venue photos from actual events. Interior empty + styled for different event types.
- Catering policy. BYO, preferred vendors, or restrictions. One clear statement, early on the page.
- Capacity breakdown. Seated, flowing reception, standing if possible.
- Named testimonials. Two or three real quotes with names and event types.

### Also needed
- AV specifics. Projector, screen, HDMI, microphone. Not just "AV amenities."
- Deposit and cancellation terms. For the pricing and FAQ sections.
- Response time commitment. Must appear before the form, not only after it.
- Parking specifics. A number, not a general statement.
- Owner name and context. Not a biography. Who is running the space.
- Floor dimensions or rough layout. Comes up often in pre-tour conversations.

## SITE STRUCTURE DECISIONS

### Home page
The home page is the surface. Every section should:
1. Answer the question each user type arrived with
2. Earn the next section — give a reason to keep scrolling
3. Leave the user wanting to know more, not already knowing everything

Home page section order:
1. Hero — what is this place, who is it for, where is it
2. Stats bar — fast trust signals (100+ events, 5 star, 100 guests, $300 starting)
3. Event type cards — let each user type see themselves. Include a next step per card.
4. Testimonial — social proof from real named people
5. Pricing — transparent numbers, no quote form
6. Amenities — scannable, not prose
7. Closing CTA — the natural end of the journey

### Inner pages
Each inner page should pick up where the home page left off on that topic.
The user already had the introduction. Skip the re-introduction.
Start from where the home page ended and go deeper.

Pricing page: User already saw the pricing preview on home. Start with the full table.
Gallery page: User already saw the event type cards. Start with the filter, then the grid.
Contact page: User already made the decision to reach out. Start with the form.

### Navigation
The nav should be useful, not just present.
Each link label should set accurate expectations about what the user will find.
"Availability" as a nav item is more useful than "Contact" for a user who just wants to check a date.
