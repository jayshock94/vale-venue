# Writing Guidelines for Website Copy

These rules apply to every piece of text written for the site. Not code. Copy. Headlines, descriptions, calls to action, page sections, photo captions, FAQ answers. All of it.

The goal is simple: sound like a real person wrote it. Warm, direct, honest. Zero corporate polish.

---

## Voice

Write like someone who actually runs this business. Not a marketing department. Not a committee.

Use contractions. Use "you" and "we." Keep it honest. If something costs extra, say so plainly. If something varies, explain what changes it. Do not dress things up with filler language.

Write the way you would explain something to a friend over coffee. That is the bar.

---

## Grammar Rules

These are non-negotiable. Every line of copy must follow them.

**No em dashes.** Not one. Not "for emphasis." Not "to add a pause." Use a comma, a period, or parentheses instead. The only exception is when directly quoting someone whose original words already contained one.

**Use commas, not semicolons.** Semicolons feel academic. This is a website, not a thesis.

**Contractions are expected.** "We're" not "We are." "You'll" not "You will." "It's" not "It is." The copy should read like speech.

**Sentence fragments are fine when they serve rhythm.** "Over 200 spots. All ours." That works. Use them sparingly.

**One exclamation mark per page, maximum.** And only if it earns it. Most of the time, a period is stronger.

**Use the Oxford comma.** Keep it consistent across the entire site.

**Periods go inside quotation marks.** Standard American English.

---

## Banned Words

These words are statistically overused by AI and make copy sound generated. Do not use any of them.

delve, utilize, leverage, facilitate, encompass, embark, endeavor, multifaceted, tapestry, testament, paradigm, synergy, holistic, catalyze, juxtapose, realm, myriad, plethora, robust, comprehensive, seamless, cutting-edge, innovative, streamline, empower, foster, enhance, elevate, optimize, scalable, pivotal, intricate, profound, resonate, underscore, harness, navigate (when used metaphorically), cultivate, bolster, galvanize, cornerstone, vibrant, dynamic, landscape (when used metaphorically), nestled, spearhead, unpack, unravel, bespoke, curated

If the right word is "use," write "use." If the right word is "help," write "help." If the right word is "big," write "big." Plain language wins every time.

---

## Banned Phrases

These phrases are AI tells. Readers flag them immediately. Never write any of these.

"In today's [anything]..." | "It's worth noting..." | "It's important to note..." | "Let's dive into..." | "Let's explore..." | "Not just X, but Y" | "At the end of the day..." | "It goes without saying..." | "Without further ado..." | "When it comes to..." | "In the realm of..." | "A [comprehensive/holistic] approach..." | "Here's the thing..." | "But here's the thing..." | "Here's what nobody tells you..." | "The best part?" | "Game-changer" | "One-stop shop" | "Whether you're planning a [list of three things]..." | "Look no further" | "Where memories are made" | "Your dream [anything] awaits" | "We pride ourselves on..." | "We go above and beyond..." | "We strive to..." | "Nestled in..." | "We're not just a [thing], we're a [bigger thing]" | "It's not about X, it's about Y" | "I'm going to be honest..." | "Let me be direct..." | "Here's the breakdown..." | "Ready to [anything]?" (as a CTA closer) | "Take your [anything] to the next level"

---

## Banned Sentence Openers

Do not begin a sentence with any of these words. Start with the actual subject instead.

Certainly | Moreover | Additionally | Furthermore | Consequently | Nevertheless | Notably | Importantly | Interestingly | Indeed | Absolutely | Undoubtedly | In fact

Just say the thing. If the next sentence matters, the reader will know. You do not need to announce it.

---

## Structural Rules

**Do not write in threes.** AI defaults to groups of three. "Whether you're looking for X, Y, or Z." That pattern signals generated text. Use two items, or four, or just describe one thing well.

**Do not write suspiciously balanced sections.** If one section needs four paragraphs and another needs two sentences, that is fine. Real information is not symmetrical.

**Do not hedge.** If something is true, state it. Do not write "can potentially accommodate" when you mean "fits." If something varies, say what it depends on. Do not soften facts with "may," "might," or "could potentially."

**Lead with the answer.** If someone wants to know about a topic, the first sentence should answer the question. Not set it up. Not provide context. Answer it, then explain.

**Vary sentence length.** Mix short sentences with longer ones. Three medium-length sentences in a row sounds robotic. Break the pattern. One sentence can be five words. The next can be twenty-five.

**No list abuse.** Do not convert every piece of information into a bulleted list. Use prose where prose is clearer. Reserve bullets for genuinely scannable content like itemized inclusions.

**No uniform paragraph length.** If every paragraph on a page is roughly the same size, rewrite some of them. Real writing is lumpy. Some ideas take one sentence. Others take a full paragraph.

---

## Punctuation Rules

**No em dashes.** Repeating this because it will be tempting every single time. Use commas or periods. Parentheses are acceptable for asides. That is all.

**Limit ellipses.** Do not trail off with "..." unless quoting someone who did. Ellipses in marketing copy feel uncertain. Be certain.

**Quotation marks for direct quotes only.** Do not use scare quotes. Do not use quotes for emphasis. If a word needs emphasis, the sentence around it should do that work.

**No excessive bolding.** Bold a word or short phrase when it genuinely helps someone scan for key information. Do not bold full sentences. Do not bold for decoration.

---

## Tone by Section Type

**Homepage / Landing:** Confident and warm. First impressions. Brief, welcoming, gets to what matters fast. No long buildups.

**About / Space Descriptions:** Honest enthusiasm. Talk about what makes things special the way someone would on a tour. Specifics over adjectives. What makes this different, not how it makes people feel.

**Pricing / Packages:** Clear and direct. No vague language. If something is an add-on, say so. If the price varies, explain what moves it. Transparency builds trust faster than polish.

**FAQ:** Short answers first, then detail if needed. Write like you are responding to a text message, not drafting a legal document.

**Gallery / Photo Sections:** Minimal copy. Let photos work. If a caption is needed, keep it to one sentence. Say what happened, not how it felt.

**Calls to Action:** Direct and specific. "Check availability" is better than "Start your journey." "See pricing" is better than "Discover your options." Tell people what clicking the button actually does.

---

## What to Avoid at the Structural Level

**Do not over-explain.** If a section heading says "Catering," you do not need to write "When it comes to catering for your special event, there are several important considerations to keep in mind." Just talk about the policy.

**Do not praise the reader.** "Great question!" and "You're smart to ask about this!" are AI habits. Just provide the answer.

**Do not write conclusions that summarize.** If a section ends, it ends. No "In summary" or "As you can see" or "We truly offer something for everyone." End on the last real point and stop.

**Do not invent statistics or testimonials.** If there is no data, do not create it. If there is no quote, do not write one. Use only real information from the knowledge base.

**Do not mirror the question in the answer.** In FAQ sections, the heading already asks the question. Do not restate it. "Can I bring my own caterer? Absolutely! You can definitely bring your own caterer." Just answer: "Yes. No restrictions on who you hire."

**Do not end sections with inspirational kickers.** "Because at the end of the day, it's all about creating moments that matter." Delete that. End with useful information.

---

## The Self-Check

Before any copy goes live, run it through these questions:

Read it out loud. Does it sound like a person talking? Or a press release?

Could you swap out the business name and have the copy work for any competitor? If yes, it is too generic. Rewrite with specifics.

Count the em dashes. The number should be zero.

Count the groups of three. Rewrite any you find.

Look at sentence openers. If three in a row start with the same structure, vary them.

Check for banned words. Even one makes the whole section feel generated.

Would the business owner read this and say, "Yeah, that sounds like us"? If the answer is not clearly yes, start over.
