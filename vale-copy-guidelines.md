# The Vale — Copy Guidelines

Upload this file at the start of every Claude Code session for this project.
Every word on this site should sound like Bobbi wrote it on a good day — not like a marketing agency, not like AI, and definitely not like every other venue site in Utah County.

---

## The voice in one sentence

Direct, warm, and quietly confident. The Vale doesn't need to sell hard because the space sells itself. The copy just needs to get out of the way and tell the truth.

---

## Who is reading this

**The bride-to-be** is searching late at night on her phone. She has looked at 12 other venues. She is emotionally exhausted from research. She does not want more marketing copy — she wants to feel something and then quickly find out if this place is within reach.

**Her mom** is helping with the research. She wants clarity, warmth, and to feel like this is a trustworthy business run by a real person.

**The corporate event planner** is scanning fast. They need three things in the first ten seconds: capacity, price range, and whether the space looks professional. They do not care about emotional language.

**The birthday/anniversary couple** wants to feel like this occasion deserves a special place. They respond to warmth and specificity over formality.

Write copy that works for all four. The way you do that: be specific, be direct, be human.

---

## The three rules that matter most

### 1. Say the specific thing, not the general thing

Bad: "A beautiful space for your special event."
Good: "Floor-to-ceiling windows facing the Wasatch. Every photo looks like a magazine."

Bad: "We offer flexible packages to suit your needs."
Good: "Three packages. Half day morning, half day evening, and full day. Pick one."

Bad: "Our venue features a range of amenities."
Good: "Dedicated bride and groom suites, a full kitchen, AV setup, and tables and chairs for 100."

The general version could describe any venue anywhere. The specific version could only describe The Vale.

### 2. Short sentences. Then a longer one when it earns it.

Most sentences should be under 12 words. Use a longer sentence when the detail justifies it. Never just to fill space.

Bad: "The Vale is a stunning contemporary event space that has been thoughtfully designed to accommodate a wide variety of events while providing a unique and memorable backdrop for your special occasion."

Good: "A modern building at the base of the Wasatch. The mountains do the rest."

### 3. Earn the period

Every sentence should end because it has said the thing. Not because you ran out of words to add. If you can cut the last three words and the sentence still works, cut them.

## Tone calibration

### Warm but not gushing
The word "stunning" is banned. So are: amazing, beautiful, breathtaking, incredible, unforgettable, magical, dream, perfect, paradise, luxury, elegant, exquisite.

These words appear on every venue site. They mean nothing. Replace them with specifics.

Instead of "stunning mountain views" — "the Wasatch mountains, right outside the windows"
Instead of "a beautiful space" — "clean lines, high ceilings, natural light"
Instead of "unforgettable memories" — "photos you will actually print"

### Confident but not arrogant
The Vale is good and it knows it. It does not need to tell you how good it is. It shows you.

Never write: "We pride ourselves on..." / "We are passionate about..." / "We are committed to..."
These phrases are filler that every business uses. They signal nothing.

Instead: just say the thing. "Pricing is on the site. No quote requests, no back-and-forth."

### Direct but not cold
Bobbi is a real person. She responds to inquiries. She is not a corporation.

First person plural is fine: "We host weddings, corporate events, and private celebrations."
Bobbi's name appears in the inquiry form context: "This goes straight to Bobbi."
Never refer to Bobbi as "our team" or "our staff" — it is her.

---

## Words and phrases to avoid

### The banned list
- stunning, beautiful, breathtaking, magical, unforgettable
- perfect, dream, paradise, luxury, elegant, exquisite
- we pride ourselves, we are passionate about, we are committed to
- a one-of-a-kind experience
- your vision
- making memories
- special day (use "your wedding" or "the reception" instead)
- tailored to your needs
- seamless experience
- state-of-the-art
- nestled (every Utah venue is "nestled")
- picturesque
- sophisticated
- upscale
- premier

### The replacement approach
Every time you would write one of those words, ask: what is the specific true thing I am replacing it with?

"Stunning views" → "The Wasatch mountains fill the windows on the north side of the building"
"Elegant space" → "High ceilings, clean white walls, concrete floors"
"Unforgettable memories" → cut the sentence entirely, it adds nothing

## Copy by section

### Hero headline
One sentence. Playfair Display Italic on the emotional word. Under 10 words.
It should answer: what is this place for, and why does it matter?

Good examples:
- "A modern space for your most important day."
- "Provo's venue with the view no one else has."
- "Clean lines. Mountain backdrop. Everything else is yours."

Bad examples:
- "Welcome to The Vale" (says nothing)
- "Where memories are made" (banned phrase)
- "Utah's premier event venue" (every venue says this)

### Hero sub-copy
2-3 sentences max. DM Sans Light. Answers the three quick questions: what is this, where is it, who is it for?

Template: [What it is physically]. [The differentiator]. [Who it serves].

Example: "Floor-to-ceiling windows. A modern building set against the Wasatch mountains. Weddings, corporate events, and private celebrations."

### Stats bar
Four facts. No decoration. Just the numbers and what they mean.

Format: number / label
- 100+ / Events hosted
- 5 star / Average review
- 100 / Guest capacity
- $300 / Starting price

Never write "over 100 events" — write "100+ events". Never write "guests" twice. Just the number and the simplest label.

### Event type cards
Each card gets: an eyebrow label, a title with one Playfair italic word, and 2-3 sentences of body copy.

The body copy answers: what makes this card type specific to The Vale?
Do not repeat information from the hero. Go deeper.

Wedding card example:
"Dedicated bride and groom suites on-site. The ceremony and reception can both happen here — no shuttling guests between venues. And the mountains behind the windows mean every photo is already halfway done."

Corporate card example:
"Clean, modern space with full AV. Comfortable for 20, manageable for 100. Half-day packages work well for retreats and offsites that don't need a full day."

Celebrations card example:
"Every milestone deserves a space that doesn't feel rented. High ceilings, real kitchen, mountain view. Birthdays and anniversaries hit different here."

### Pricing section header
Two sentences. Acknowledge that pricing is published on purpose.

Good: "No quote requests. No mystery. Every package and price is here because your time matters."
Bad: "We offer transparent pricing to suit your budget." (too corporate)

### Pricing table
Package names: short and descriptive — Half Day Morning, Half Day Evening, Full Day.
Times: exact — 8am – 2pm, 3pm – 9pm, 9am – 10pm.
Never use "starting at" language in the table itself — just the numbers.
The Full Day package gets a "Best value" badge — no other badges in the table.

### Amenity list
Plain list. No decoration. Each item starts with the noun, not a verb.

Good: "Kitchen and food prep area" / "Bride vanity and dressing suite"
Bad: "Enjoy access to our fully-equipped kitchen" / "Pamper yourself in our bridal suite"

The list should read like an honest inventory, not a sales pitch.

### Testimonial
Real words from real people. Do not clean them up too much. If the grammar is slightly informal, leave it. That is the point.

Format: quote / name and event type / month and year
Keep quotes under 40 words. The best testimonials are specific about a detail, not general about the experience.

Good: "We walked in and the mountains were right there in the windows. We stopped looking at other venues that same afternoon."
Bad: "The Vale was the perfect venue for our special day. We highly recommend it to anyone looking for a beautiful space."

### Inquiry form
The heading is not "Contact Us". It is "Tell us about your event."

The sub-copy is short and direct: "No runaround. Pricing is on the site. This goes straight to Bobbi."

Form field labels: Full name / Email address / Event type / Desired date / Tell us more
The "Tell us more" placeholder: "Guest count, vision, anything else we should know."

The submit button: "Send Inquiry" — not "Submit" or "Get in touch" or "Let's talk"

Confirmation message: "Bobbi will respond within one business day."

### Footer tagline
One sentence. Under 15 words. Factual.
Good: "A modern event space at the base of the Wasatch mountains. Provo, Utah."

## Numbers and facts — always be specific

Use real numbers wherever possible:
- Not "ample parking" — "parking for 40+ vehicles"
- Not "flexible capacity" — "up to 100 guests seated"
- Not "affordable pricing" — "packages starting at $300"
- Not "quick response" — "responds within one business day"

When you write a placeholder, make it a realistic placeholder:
Not [insert testimonial here] — write a dummy testimonial that sounds like a real person wrote it.

## Mobile copy

On mobile, every section heading should work as a standalone sentence when the layout collapses. Test this: read just the headings in sequence. Do they tell the story of The Vale without the body copy? They should.

Section heading sequence:
1. "A modern space for your most important day."
2. "Every kind of occasion."
3. "We walked in and just knew."
4. "Transparent pricing."
5. "Everything included."
6. "Ready to see the space?"

That sequence should work as an advertisement on its own.

---

## What AI copy sounds like — avoid all of this

AI copy patterns to never use:
- Starting a sentence with "Whether you're..."
- The phrase "Look no further"
- "In today's world..."
- Any sentence that could apply to a different business with one word swapped
- Lists of three adjectives separated by commas: "elegant, modern, versatile"
- Ending with "Contact us today to learn more"
- "We look forward to hearing from you"
- Any use of the word "journey" to describe booking a venue

The test: read the sentence aloud. Does it sound like something a real person would actually say to another person? If not, rewrite it.

---

## The final test

Before finalizing any copy, ask these three questions:

1. Could this sentence appear on any other venue's website? If yes, make it more specific.
2. Does this sentence contain a word from the banned list? If yes, replace it with a specific truth.
3. Can I cut three words from this sentence without losing meaning? If yes, cut them.

Copy that passes all three is ready.
